## Structure

Section 3 should now contain five subsections, each modeled on the ERA LinkedIn Audit format (see Brian Gonsalves audit at eracx.com/audit/brian-gonsalves for visual reference). Each subsection gets a profile card, key metrics, dimension scores, and a "Three things that will move the needle" finding.

### Subsection layout per GTM channel:

**Profile card** (dark background, name, title, follower count)

**Hero metrics row** (4 cards):
- Posts (with spark bar showing cadence)
- Engagement (avg reactions per post)
- ICP Match (% of commenters who match buyer persona)
- Original Content (count of non-repost, non-event posts)

**Dimension gauges** (adapt from Brian's audit, scored A-D):
- Authority: Does this person's content position them as an expert the buyer would trust?
- Trust: Is the content building trust with the buyer, or performing for the internal audience?
- Distribution: Is the content reaching beyond the existing network into the buyer's feed?
- Relationship: Is the person engaging with buyer-level contacts?
- Funnel Mix: Is there a balance of awareness / consideration / decision content?
- Business Impact: Does the content contain proof points, ROI data, or client outcomes?

**Key finding callout** (one sentence, prominent)

**"If you read only this"** (three actions, prioritized, specific)

## The Five GTM Channels

### Channel 1: CEO (Alexi Robichaux)
**Score: 24/100**

Hero metrics:
- Posts: ~11 visible in last 12 months (event-driven, long gaps)
- Avg engagement: 270 reactions per post
- ICP match: 6% of commenters
- Original content: 2 posts (Workslop research, Human+AI launch)

Dimension scores:
- Authority: C (62) — recognized as category creator, but content doesn't demonstrate enterprise expertise
- Trust: D (30) — content builds brand awareness, not buyer trust
- Distribution: D (25) — engagement is internal orbit, not reaching buyer feeds
- Relationship: D (20) — comment behavior is 62% internal, 0% buyer engagement
- Funnel Mix: F (15) — 100% top-of-funnel, zero consideration or decision content
- Business Impact: D (20) — zero ROI data, zero client outcomes with metrics

Key finding: "Alexi's conviction is real. His attention is inward. 62% of his comment engagement goes to current or former BetterUp employees. The enterprise buyer who would write a $2M check does not appear in his engagement data."

Three things:
1. Shift 60% of content from philosophy to outcome language. Every post about "human potential" needs a companion post about retention rates, manager effectiveness, or coaching ROI.
2. Build a CHRO 100 list. 10 strategic connection requests per week to CHROs at target accounts. Comment substantively on their content before expecting them to engage with his.
3. Stop congratulating departures. Every "Congrats on the new role!" comment on a former employee's departure post reinforces the turnover narrative that Glassdoor already documents.

## Moodlight Audience Integration Points

Weave these findings from the Moodlight audience intelligence report into Section 3:

**On why the GTM chain matters:**
> The enterprise buyer BetterUp targets "lives in curated information feeds, not social discourse." They "consume in private, decide in committee, and share only what makes them look informed, not enthusiastic." This means the buyer will evaluate BetterUp's people on LinkedIn as part of their due diligence, but they will not engage visibly. The GTM team must be producing content the buyer would save and share internally, not content that generates public likes.

**On why philosophical content fails:**
> The Moodlight data shows 84% of the conversation around BetterUp's category is emotionally neutral. Curiosity is the only positive emotion with meaningful volume, and even it's "cool curiosity — the kind that reads the article but doesn't share it." The buyer's dominant emotional state is "studied indifference masking deep uncertainty." Philosophical content from the CEO does not penetrate this emotional lockdown. Proof content does.

**On what the buyer actually wants to see:**
> The audience "speaks in operational language, not aspirational language." They signal trust through: "Measurable impact on manager effectiveness," "Consolidated tech stack," "Integration with our existing HRIS," "Time-to-productivity for new managers," "We replaced three vendors with one platform." The GTM team is speaking none of this language on LinkedIn.

**On the audience's platform behavior:**
> The buyer "will not share content that makes them sound like they drank the coaching Kool-Aid. They will share content that makes them sound like a sharp operator who found an efficiency." Every piece of LinkedIn content from BetterUp's GTM team should pass this test: would a CHRO share this to look smart in front of their CFO?
