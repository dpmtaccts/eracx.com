# Stephen Roesler LinkedIn voice and content

This skill captures how Stephen actually talks and how he wants to show up on LinkedIn. It is built from the April 17 Justin/Stephen call transcript, the Pro-Viz article, the 2025 annual letter, and Stephen's own LinkedIn strategy notes. When in doubt, follow the transcript, not what would seem polished.

## Who Stephen is on LinkedIn

A working production company co-founder. Forty hours a week on actual sets and edits. Posts when something on a job sparks a thought, not when a content schedule says it is time to post.

He is not a thought leader. He is a person with a real chair in the room who sometimes writes about what happens in it.

The audience he is writing for: in-house creative directors, ECDs, CMOs, VPs of creative, and producers at large B2C brands. They have seen every flavor of agency LinkedIn post. They will smell a manifesto from the first line and scroll past it.

## Opening

Stephen does not open with a thesis. He opens with an observation, often a number, often a moment that just happened.

- **Lead with the small specific thing, not the big idea.** "Spent a Tuesday afternoon in an edit suite watching a director and an editor argue over a three-frame cut." That is the open. The lesson comes later, if at all.
- **Question to himself, then answer it.** From the call: "Should I be on LinkedIn, yes or no? It's like the answer's pretty obvious, right? If yes, then. Then what?" That self-questioning rhythm is a Stephen tell. Use it once per post when it fits.
- **A line repeated to oneself.** "A line I keep repeating to myself this year: a lot has changed, nothing has changed." Confessional opening, lowers the audience's defenses.
- **Specific scene, no setup.** "Eight people, one long table, no agenda, four hours." No throat-clearing. The image is the open.

What Stephen does not do at the open: ask a rhetorical "did you know?" question, name a trend, frame a "why this matters." He earns the right to a thesis by getting to it three paragraphs in.

## Voice and texture

Stephen's voice has specific tells from the transcript. Preserve them.

- **Hedges with confidence.** "I think." "My hunch is." "Probably not, ever." "I do think that." These are not weakness markers. Stephen hedges constantly on real calls. Cutting them all makes him sound like a different person. Keep one or two per post.
- **Restarts and rephrases.** Stephen will start a thought, abandon it mid-sentence, and come back from a different angle. On LinkedIn this becomes: a sentence that ends, then a second sentence that picks up the same idea differently. "It works because it solves an old problem, not because it is new. The boardroom does not need more AI. It needs more certainty."
- **Casual texture, controlled dose.** "The level-set is the whole point." "Honest answer." "Best version of this job." One per post, never stacked. Stephen swears in person but he does not need to on LinkedIn.
- **Self-deprecation that is not self-deprecating.** "I have repeated it to four clients since." "I never wanted to be an email guy." Acknowledges the absurdity of the situation without performing humility.
- **References he actually reads.** The Rundown AI digest. The ARC-AGI test. JEPA / Yann LeCun. These are the things Stephen actually consumes. They become natural anchors. Do not invent references he does not use.

## What Stephen does NOT do

- **No em dashes.** Period. Restructure the sentence with periods, commas, or colons.
- **No three-or-more short declarative sentences in a row.** "It worked. It changed everything. We won." That is the AI tell. Two short sentences is the ceiling.
- **No buzzwords.** Synergy, leverage, unlock, paradigm shift, game-changer, 10x. If a word would appear in a generic LinkedIn post, find a different word.
- **No filler intros.** "Hope you're well." "Just wanted to share." "I have been thinking about." Cut.
- **No business-tie-in endings.** Do not bolt "and that's why X is the future" onto the end of a post about an edit suite. End on the observation. The audience makes the leap.
- **No call to action endings.** "What do you think?" "Drop a comment." "DM me." Stephen would not do this and the audience does not need it. The exception is the AICP Week community post, where "drop their name below" is in-character because it actually invites useful answers.
- **No thought-leadership framing.** "Here are 5 lessons from..." "The 3 things every CMO needs to know about..." Never. Stephen earns the lesson by the time he gets to it.
- **No serialization.** Do not number "1/" "2/" through a thread. Stephen posts standalone observations, not LinkedIn carousels with a numbered hook.

## Sentence rhythm

- **Mostly medium-length sentences.** Stephen on the call: "I write longer sentences with commas. I like to breathe and think. I think like that." Honor that.
- **A short sentence works because it sits next to longer ones.** Variety holds attention. Three short ones in a row does not.
- **End paragraphs on the strongest line.** Stephen lands ideas sideways, but he lands them. The last sentence of each paragraph is the one that stays.

## Closing

Stephen's endings do not perform. They land.

- **End on the observation, not the lesson.** "That conversation is the film." "It needs more certainty." "See you there." The reader carries the idea.
- **Or end on a small concession.** "If I missed someone obvious, drop their name below." Lowers the temperature, signals he is human.
- **No "what this means for your business" tie-in.** Trust the reader to make the leap.

## Format mix and proportions

Stephen's stated time budget is one substantial article a quarter, "honestly, one a month." So the calendar leans on lighter formats. Default mix per ten posts:

| Format | Count | Stephen's lift | What it does |
|--------|-------|----------------|--------------|
| Original post (deep idea) | 3 | 30 to 45 min each | Plants the flag on Pro-Viz and the work |
| Comment or repost with a take | 3 | 5 to 10 min each | Builds presence in other people's threads |
| Texture post (set, dinner, photo) | 2 | 5 min each | Humanizes Stephen, lowers the taste bar safely |
| AICP Week orbit (community + landing) | 2 | 20 min each | Pays out social capital before walking into AICP |

If a deep idea post takes more than 45 minutes of Stephen's time, the engine is producing too clean a draft. Stop and rebuild from a smaller observation.

## The three pillars Stephen named

From the April 17 transcript, Stephen drew the boundary around three categories. Use these as the universe.

### 1. AI, AGI, and filmmaking
"Tough to be proven wrong because it is happening in real time." Anything from the Rundown AI digest, capability tests (ARC-AGI), model releases (Sora 2, Veo 3), JEPA-style technical references, real production lessons using AI. Stephen can defend any take here because he is in the work daily.

### 2. The craft behind the pixel
Three frames in an edit suite. The thirty days between approval and bid. A director's call on a lens choice. The VFX artist as environment architect. Specific, observable, drawn from a real job. This is the safest pillar because it cannot be faked.

### 3. Texture posts and dinner photos
"A photo of the actual set, but you can take some kind of cryptic, you know, whatever and post that. People will be like where is that?" An empty stage, a monitor village, a candlelit dinner table, a venue Stephen tagged once. Caption is one or two sentences. No name-drops unless the attendee approved.

Stephen explicitly excluded a fourth pillar: standard agency-LinkedIn fare like new-hire announcements, project wins, awards posts, panel invites. Those are Austin Barbera's beat. Not Stephen's.

## Voice calibration checklist

Before sending a draft to Justin or Stephen, ask:

1. Does the opening sound like Stephen noticing something, or like he is giving a TED talk? If TED talk, rewrite the open.
2. Are there any em dashes? If yes, restructure.
3. Are there three short declarative sentences in a row anywhere? If yes, fold the third into a longer line or cut it.
4. Does the post end on a business analogy or a "what this means for your industry" tie-in? If yes, cut the last paragraph entirely.
5. Did I use specific numbers, specific scenes, and specific names where they earn it, or did I generalize?
6. Is there at least one hedge ("I think," "my hunch is," "probably") that signals this is Stephen, not a brand voice?
7. If I borrowed a reference (Rundown AI, ARC-AGI, JEPA, a directors' guild line), is it one Stephen actually engages with, or did I invent a credibility signal?
8. Would Chris Bellinger or Greg Hahn read this and recognize a peer, or would they recognize agency content?

## Common AI tells to scrub

These patterns signal AI-written copy. Stephen will catch them and Justin will flag them.

- "It's not just X, it's Y." Lazy contrast structure.
- "Here's the thing..." / "The truth is..." Empty preamble.
- "Game-changer," "paradigm shift," "next-level." Buzzword tells.
- "In conclusion," "to summarize," "the bottom line is." You do not need them.
- Excessive em dashes used as a rhythm crutch.
- Three short declarative sentences in a row for "punch."
- A business analogy bolted onto a craft observation.
- Smooth, evenly-paced prose with no rhythmic variety.
- "As someone who..." or "Having spent X years in...". Stephen does not need to credentialize. The observation does that.
- Numbered lists ("3 lessons from...") in a Stephen post. Save numbered lists for actual frameworks; never as a hook.

## Tone defaults

- Observational, not declarative. Stephen earns the right to declare by getting there through specifics.
- Confident without flexing. The work is the credibility, not the language.
- Self-aware about his own posture. "I never wanted to be an email guy." Lets the audience trust the next paragraph.
- Takes the audience seriously. They are CMOs, ECDs, VPs of creative. Do not over-explain. Do not condescend.
- Slightly amused. Stephen on the call laughs at his own restarts. That dryness should show up once or twice per post.

## Format-specific rules

### Original posts (deep idea)
- 1,000 to 1,800 characters. Long enough to develop an idea, short enough to read on mobile.
- First three lines must earn the "see more" click. That is the truncation point.
- Three to six paragraphs. Mostly two to four sentences each.
- One specific scene or number anchoring the whole thing.
- Lesson stated once at the end, never repeated.

### Reposts with a take
- Stephen's add is 60 to 150 words.
- Lead with the level-set: "the part most coverage skips," "the honest answer is," "the actual gap is."
- Always name the specific source (the Rundown AI, the ARC-AGI test, the LeCun talk). The specificity is half the value.
- End on the human counterweight: "a person with twenty years of experience standing next to it." This is Stephen's signature move on AI content.

### Comments
- 30 to 80 words.
- Add to the original poster's idea, do not redirect to Miniac. The whole point is appearing in their thread, not converting it.
- One specific observation, not a list of points.
- No CTAs, no DM-me, no link to your website.

### Texture posts
- One to two sentences.
- Photo carries 80% of the post.
- No name-drops unless the attendee or client approved.
- Acceptable subjects: empty set, monitor village before crew call, dim edit suite, dinner table, venue exterior, hotel hallway in a city Stephen is shooting in. Not acceptable: Stephen's face front and center, agency-style headshots, group photos with anyone tagged.

## When to break these rules

These are defaults, not laws. Stephen will break any of them when the topic demands it. The rule is to break them deliberately, not accidentally. If a draft uses an em dash, opens with a thesis, or ends on a business tie-in, there should be a reason, and Justin should be able to articulate it.
